First 24-36 Hours After Arrest
Architecture, Urban Design, Theory
2017

The prohibition of photography in the interiors of police stations, secured spaces within courts, and jails makes it difficult to fully describe the processes of arrest, arraignment, and detainment. Understanding the nuances of these processes and details of the locations they happen within is critical to understanding the specific ways in which they disproportionately disadvantage the poor and people of color. This drawing of the processes, people, and spaces a person encounters after their arrest was gathered from General Services Administration design guides and from interviews with public defenders, legal aid representatives, the Center Court for Innovation, and jail and court architects. This drawing was done in collaboration with Stella Ioannidou.

Expanding Gers, Encompassing Neighbors
Architecture, Urban Design, Theory
2017

The harsh conditions in the densifying ger districts in Ulaanbaatar, Mongolia caused by a lack of waste, water, and heat infrastructures are compounded by the cultural newness of sedentary life for most residents. From nomadic to sedentary, the social benefits of the ger have not extended from the steppe to the city. Structurally autonomous, transportable, and with a low occupant capacity - the ger has worked well for nomads for millennia, however the need for community organizing and networking has increased as the problems created by proximity and density grow. The creation of larger span spaces using the existing housing typology creates a low cost way for residents to create these spaces themselves as the central government is increasingly unable to finance new development. This project seeks to both prototype new components for the ger and ways of manufacturing them so residents can build these spaces and sell the manufactured components themselves.

Repositioning The Court
Architecture, Urban Design, Theory
2016

Approximately 80% of the persons being held at Rikers Island have not been charged with a crime. The rigid criminal justice system in New York City provides few opportunities for an individual (especially a poor individual or a person of color) to break loose from the cycle of incarceration after being arrested, regardless of the crime. In addition to crucial new policies on bail and funding for diversion to services, the spaces within the criminal justice system need to be designed to facilitate these policies and provide flexibility to accommodate alternative juridical processes that prevent the poor from being detained without a conviction.
